The document outlines the challenges and policies related to the health protection and surveillance of federal civilian personnel deployed to Afghanistan and Iraq. These personnel, including those from the Total Force concept—comprising active duty military, reservists, defense contractors, host nation personnel, and DOD federal civilians—are deployed in operations such as Joint Endeavor, Joint Guardian, and Desert Storm, and have since participated in combat support functions in Enduring Freedom and Iraqi Freedom. DOD relies on these personnel for critical missions such as intelligence collection, criminal investigations, and weapon systems maintenance.

Federal civilian personnel are designated under the emergency-essential program, which identifies those whose roles are essential for combat operations or critical systems. DOD can deploy these personnel voluntarily or involuntarily. However, the department lacks clear procedures to ensure full compliance with force health protection and surveillance policies, and records show that some components failed to meet policy requirements for deployed personnel in Afghanistan and Iraq.

The document highlights that DOD did not require centralized collection of data on the identity, movements, and health status of deployed civilians, which limited its ability to assess the effectiveness of its health protection and surveillance capabilities. In August 2006, DOD issued a revised policy to address this gap, but the procedures were insufficient to ensure compliance. Components reviewed lacked documentation showing consistent implementation of pre-deployment health assessments, immunizations, tuberculosis and HIV screenings, and post-deployment health assessments.

The policies mandate that deploying civilian personnel complete pre-deployment health assessments within 30 days of deployment, with healthcare providers reviewing these assessments to confirm readiness and identify additional evaluations. However, the reviewed components did not provide sufficient evidence of consistent compliance with these requirements.

A review of deployment records found that, out of 3,771 records, only 52 were complete, with documentation missing for 19 (Navy) and 29 (Air Force) records. In contrast, all three Army locations had complete records, indicating that most deployed civilians received pre-deployment health assessments. DOD policies require theater-specific immunizations for all deployed civilian personnel, including hepatitis A, tetanus-diphtheria, smallpox, typhoid, and influenza. However, documentation of these assessments varied significantly by location, ranging from less than 1% to over 90%.
