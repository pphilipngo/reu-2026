The U.S. Department of Defense (DOD) is responsible for overseeing the health protection and surveillance of federal civilian personnel deployed to Afghanistan and Iraq. This includes ensuring compliance with pre- and post-deployment medical assessments, immunizations, and health monitoring policies, which are essential for maintaining the readiness and safety of deployed civilians. These policies support the broader mission of supporting combat operations and ensuring the well-being of those who serve.

The DOD established the emergency-essential program in 1985 to deploy civilian personnel for critical missions, but has faced challenges in enforcing force health protection requirements. While DOD components have implemented policies requiring pre-deployment health assessments, immunizations, and post-deployment health evaluations, documentation of these assessments is inconsistent across locations. For example, only 52 out of 3,771 records show that federal civilians received required health assessments, with varying levels of documentation across different components.

The DOD mandates specific immunizations, such as hepatitis A, tetanus-diphtheria, and influenza, based on deployment locations, but some locations lack complete records of these immunizations. Post-deployment health assessments, including tuberculosis and HIV screenings, are required, yet documentation of these assessments is sparse, with only 3,525 out of 3,771 deployed civilians having records of these assessments upon returning to the U.S.

DOD has also struggled with collecting centralized data on the health status of deployed civilians, leading to gaps in monitoring and assessing the effectiveness of its health protection policies. The lack of a centralized system for tracking health data and the absence of clear guidance for components to report location-specific information hinder the DOD’s ability to address health concerns and ensure compliance with force health protection requirements.

In response, the DOD issued revised policies in 2006 to improve data collection and oversight, but these measures have not fully resolved the issues. The Department of Defense has emphasized the importance of medical treatment policies for deployed civilians, which include levels of care from basic first aid to hospital-level treatment. While DOD federal civilians receive care through the military health system, they are eligible for workers’ compensation claims, which are processed by the Department of Labor’s Office of Workers’ Compensation Programs (OWCP). These claims are evaluated based on evidence from DOD civilian staff, and while DOD policies are generally followed, inconsistencies in documentation and compliance persist.

The review highlights the need for a more robust oversight and quality assurance mechanism to ensure that deployed federal civilians receive the necessary medical assessments and immunizations. DOD has acknowledged the importance of these policies and has taken steps to improve data collection and documentation, but challenges remain in fully implementing these requirements across all components. The document underscores the critical role of federal civilian personnel in supporting DOD operations and the need for consistent, reliable health monitoring to protect their well-being and ensure mission success.
