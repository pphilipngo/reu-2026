The U.S. federal tax system is structured to collect revenue from both corporate and individual income, with distinct mechanisms for each. Corporate income is taxed at the entity level, with profits distributed as dividends or capital gains subject to individual tax rates. Pass-through entities, such as partnerships and LLCs, are taxed at the individual level when their income is passed through to shareholders. The federal corporate income tax base includes net business income, interest, dividends, rent, royalties, and capital gains, with a tax rate ranging from 15% to 35%. The U.S. taxes worldwide income of domestic corporations, but provides a foreign tax credit to avoid double taxation.

The individual income tax system is based on the source of income, with U.S.- and foreign earnings subject to taxation. Statutory rates range from 10% to 35%, with lower rates applied to long-term capital gains and dividend income. Sole proprietors pay both employer and employee social insurance taxes on their business income. U.S. citizens and residents are taxed on worldwide income, including foreign-source dividends and capital gains, with a credit for foreign taxes paid. The system is evaluated for economic efficiency, equity, and simplicity, though trade-offs often exist between these factors.

Business taxation is a key source of federal revenue, contributing $278 billion in 2005. Business-source income, such as dividends, capital gains, and rental income, accounts for 14–19% of individual income. Businesses pay most of the remaining federal taxes, making them vital to addressing fiscal challenges. However, the system is complex and inefficient, leading to compliance costs and equity issues. Tax policies can create economic inefficiencies by directing investments toward lightly taxed activities, shifting resources away from more productive uses.

The tax system’s complexity and frequent changes make compliance difficult and costly, with estimated annual compliance costs between $40 billion and $85 billion. The business income tax gap, the difference between actual and legally required payments, is significant, with corporations contributing $32 billion to the gap. The complexity of the tax code, intricate business transactions, and multinational structures make determining tax liabilities and avoidance strategies challenging. Corporate tax avoidance has become a major concern, with some companies using the tax code to maximize shareholder value, leading to the emergence of "profit centers" within tax departments.

Transfer pricing in multinational corporations is a challenge, as determining the "arm’s length" price for goods, services, or intangible assets is difficult due to the global economy and complex operations. Transfer pricing disputes are likely to increase as more companies operate internationally. Tax shelters, while sometimes legal, can be abusive if they manipulate tax codes or regulations, often hidden in complex tax returns.

The U.S. tax system’s complexity and frequent changes erode public confidence, with about 10% of respondents finding it acceptable to cheat on taxes. Strengthening enforcement is essential to build confidence in fairness, particularly for businesses and wage earners. Transparency in tax administration, including public access to reconciliation between book and tax income, is critical. Reforming the business tax system requires careful design choices, such as whether the system should be closer to a pure income tax or a consumption tax, how to tax foreign business sales, and whether to tax businesses or individuals.

The distinction between income and consumption taxes is important for understanding how savings and investment are treated. A pure income tax includes all income, while a pure consumption tax excludes income used for saving and investment. The current U.S. system is a hybrid, taxing some savings and investment but not others. Economists agree that the tax system’s design reflects a balance between efficiency, equity, and simplicity, though trade-offs are inevitable.
