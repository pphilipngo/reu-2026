The Medicare payment system for Ambulatory Surgical Centers (ASCs) and hospital outpatient departments differs in how it calculates and adjusts payment rates for procedures. ASCs are paid based on the number of procedures performed, with the highest-paying procedure receiving 100% of the rate and each additional procedure receiving 50%. Medicare covers direct costs like nursing, drugs, supplies, anesthesia, and diagnostics, but not implantable devices or professional services billed by physicians or anesthesiologists. ASCs bill separately for these items.

The Prospective Payment System (OPPS), implemented in 1983 for hospital outpatient services, assigns payment rates to specific procedures, not a fixed number of procedures. Hospitals receive 100% of the payment for the procedure with the highest rate and 50% for each additional procedure, with exceptions. The OPPS aims to cover the costs of procedures performed by hospitals.

ASCs, under the OPPS, are paid based on the number of procedures, with the highest-paying procedure receiving 100% of the rate and additional procedures receiving 50%. The payment system for ASCs was established in 1982, based on data from 40 ASCs, with 125 in operation at that time. Procedures were grouped into four payment groups, and rates were adjusted over time, increasing the number of groups to eight by 1990 and adding a ninth group in 1991.

The payment structures for hospital outpatient services under Medicare are compared to the ASC payment system. Medicare payments for procedures do not include physician or nonphysician practitioner services, which are billed separately. The Office of the Physician Service (OPPS) allows hospitals to bill for certain additional services, such as drugs and diagnostic tests, but not for implantable devices related to the procedure. Hospitals may also bill for non-related services like laboratory tests. CMS encourages hospitals to report all services during a procedure, leading to more detailed claims.

The 2003 MMA eliminated the 5-year survey requirement and mandated a revised ASC payment system by January 1, 2008. In 2006, CMS published a proposed rule to revise the ASC payment system based on OPPS APC groups, but with lower rates for ASCs. OPPS payment rates, implemented in 2000, were based on hospitals' 1996 costs, calculated using cost-to-charge ratios from recent data. Median costs for each APC group were determined, and these were used to assign payment rates.
