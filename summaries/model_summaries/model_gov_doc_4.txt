The IRS, responsible for administering federal tax laws and providing taxpayer services, has faced significant challenges in maintaining the security and integrity of its operations. In fiscal year 2015, the agency collected over $3.3 trillion in taxes, processed more than 243 million tax returns, and issued over $403 billion in tax refunds, employing approximately 90,000 staff across 550 offices. These operations exposed vulnerabilities, particularly in information security, as two major data breaches occurred in 2015 and 2016, with unauthorized third parties accessing taxpayer information through the Get Transcript application. This led to the exposure of sensitive data, including Social Security numbers, birth dates, and addresses, affecting over 220,000 accounts by February 2016.

The IRS has implemented policies to enhance identification and authentication, such as multifactor authentication, password complexity requirements, and automated password management. However, these controls are not fully effective, with issues like the use of easily guessable passwords on key systems and excessive access permissions for certain applications. Cryptographic controls, including encryption for user authentication data, were not fully implemented, and audit logging for two critical systems was lacking. These deficiencies highlight the need for continuous improvement in access controls and monitoring.

The IRS has also struggled with inconsistent implementation of information security policies, particularly in configuration management and system maintenance. While the agency has developed policies for IT systems, it has not ensured timely application of security patches to critical databases, including one from 2012. Despite these efforts, control weaknesses are attributed to inconsistent implementation of the agency’s information security program. The IRS has established a comprehensive framework for information security, but ongoing challenges remain in addressing new and existing vulnerabilities.

The IRS has identified several ongoing and unresolved security deficiencies, including inadequate auditing and monitoring of access, failure to address prior recommendations, and insufficient implementation of corrective actions. These deficiencies, combined with new issues identified in fiscal year 2015, constitute a significant deficiency in the IRS’s internal control over financial reporting systems. The agency has received new recommendations to enhance its information security program and 43 technical recommendations to address new access control and configuration management weaknesses. Implementing these recommendations, along with 49 outstanding prior recommendations, is essential to improving user authentication, access limitation, data protection, auditing, and physical security of IT resources.
