The U.S. Immigration and Naturalization Service (INS) has encountered persistent challenges in managing its operations, including issues related to organizational structure, program implementation, information systems, and staff training. Historically, the INS was decentralized, leading to geographical separation of programs, poor resource allocation, and inconsistent implementation. This structure resulted in a single senior manager overseeing a large number of field directors and Border Patrol chiefs, contributing to inefficiencies. Reforms in 1994 aimed to address the operations office's large span of control and its focus on matters that should have been handled by field managers, but the organizational structure remains unstable, with ongoing challenges in clarifying responsibilities between the Office of Field Operations and the Office of Programs.

Program implementation faces challenges, particularly in visa processing for specialty occupations. The focus on quantity over quality in adjudicator performance evaluations—measured by the number of applications reviewed—led to ineffective enforcement, with adjudicators pressured to process cases quickly, reducing the use of investigative resources to detect fraud. Despite this, fraud was identified, including the creation of shell corporations and false documents for ineligible aliens. The May 2001 report noted that INS grants work authorization to applicants for adjustment of status before adjudication, but in fiscal year 2000, about 80,000 applications were denied, leading to revocations.

The INS also struggles with managing work authorization, naturalization, and immigration benefit fraud. Naturalization processing was found to have quality and integrity issues, with ineligible applicants receiving citizenship. The Department of Justice OIG report found that INS prioritized timely naturalization application completion over accuracy, leading to fraud. To address this, quality control procedures were implemented. However, similar issues were noted in the review of INS' efforts to combat immigration benefit fraud, with findings that INS struggled to balance enforcement and service delivery. The Visa Waiver Program, which allows entry with a passport, is criticized for being vulnerable to abuse, with stolen passports used for illegal entry. An OIG report found 27 stolen Icelandic passports used in such cases, underscoring the threat to national security and illegal immigration.

Information technology challenges hinder efficiency in processing applications. The INS maintains automated and manual systems for program and management data, but these systems are not well-integrated or compatible, leading to inefficiencies and significant financial investments not being effectively utilized. The lack of a cohesive information technology strategy continues to impede the INS's ability to streamline operations and improve service delivery.
