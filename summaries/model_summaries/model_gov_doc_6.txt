The document provides a detailed overview of the environmental and health impacts of trichloroethylene (TCE) and perchlorate, focusing on their classification, exposure pathways, and regulatory responses. TCE, classified as a DOD-emerging contaminant, is a colorless liquid with a chloroform-like odor, used in industrial and consumer products. It has a maximum contaminant level (MCL) of 5 ppb in drinking water under the Safe Drinking Water Act, but its use in cosmetics, dry-cleaning, and adhesives has been discontinued. TCE is widely present in the environment, primarily through industrial wastewater, and is a significant source of exposure via inhalation. The Department of Defense (DOD) has used TCE in industrial processes, including cleaning sensitive equipment, and has faced scrutiny over its long-term health effects, particularly in contaminated areas like Camp Lejeune.

Perchlorate, another DOD-classified contaminant, is a primary ingredient in propellants and has been used by the DOD, NASA, and the defense industry. It is readily dissolved and transported in water, found in groundwater, surface water, and soil. While the EPA set a maximum contaminant limit (MCL) for TCE in 1989, it has not established a standard for perchlorate under the Safe Drinking Water Act. Scientific concerns about perchlorate’s potential carcinogenic effects, including its classification by the National Toxicology Program and the International Agency for Research on Cancer, have led to increased scrutiny. The FDA has tested food items for perchlorate and has proposed a 2007 assessment of its exposure from foods, while the CDC has highlighted iodine deficiencies linked to thyroid hormone disruption.

Both TCE and perchlorate are associated with significant health risks, particularly for infants, fetuses, and adults. TCE exposure has been linked to nervous system effects, liver and lung damage, and potential cancer risks, with the Agency for Toxic Substances and Disease Registry (ATSDR) noting a 1991 public health assessment of its exposure at Camp Lejeune. Perchlorate has been linked to thyroid dysfunction and potential cancer risks, with the EPA setting a reference dose of 0.0007 mg/kg/day, translating to a drinking water equivalent level (DWEL) of 24.5 ppb. Despite regulatory efforts, concerns about long-term health effects have led to ongoing studies and legal claims, particularly in areas with historical contamination.
