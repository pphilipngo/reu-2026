The U.S. Census Bureau is modernizing its 2020 Census operations to reduce costs and improve efficiency through innovative methods and technologies. The increasing complexity of the population and the growing diversity of the census population have led to higher costs over the past four decades. The 2010 Census, costing $13 billion, was 56% more expensive than the 2000 Census, which cost $8.1 billion. To address these challenges, the Bureau is exploring new approaches, including reengineering address canvassing, optimizing self-response through Internet options, and leveraging administrative records to minimize follow-up work. These efforts are part of a broader initiative to redesign the census, with a focus on reducing staffing, infrastructure, and field operations.

The Bureau conducted multiple field tests to evaluate potential redesigns, including self-response and non-response follow-up methods, field operations management systems, and mobile device use for data collection. These tests informed the 2020 Census Operational Plan, which includes 326 design decision points. The Bureau has committed to implementing four key redesign areas: 100% address canvassing in the office, targeting 25% of addresses for in-the-field canvassing, offering self-response options via the Internet, allowing mobile device use for enumerators, using administrative records to enumerate vacant units, and leveraging enterprise solutions for the 2020 Census. These decisions are expected to save $5.2 billion, with the 2010 Census redesign estimated to cost $13 billion, and the successful implementation of the four redesign areas projected to cost $12.5 billion.

The Bureau is modernizing its survey data collection and processing functions through an enterprise-wide IT initiative called Census Enterprise Data Collection and Processing (CEDCAP). CEDCAP aims to replace multiple, redundant survey-specific systems with a unified system-of-systems approach. Key components include a dynamic operational control system for field work, real-time data tracking, and automated route planning. CEDCAP also explores the use of mobile devices for data collection and develops a web-based survey application to support increased data processing and storage. The initiative consists of 12 projects to be implemented over 10 releases, with capabilities rolled out incrementally for the 2020 Census. The program is projected to cost as of August 2015.
