The document outlines the management of conventional ammunition within the U.S. Department of Defense (DOD), focusing on demilitarization processes, inventory tracking, and cross-leveling strategies. The Army, as the Single Manager for Conventional Ammunition (SMCA), oversees the demilitarization of all conventional ammunition, including non-SMCA-managed items. Services assess inventory to identify unserviceable or excess ammunition, which is then transferred to the SMCA's demilitarization account through an annual cross-leveling process. The demilitarization process involves incorporating ammunition into the CAD stockpile, with specific depots and methods detailed in Appendix III. The Secretary of the Army serves as the DOD's SMCA, ensuring the life cycle management of conventional ammunition from research to disposal.

The Program Executive Office for Ammunition (PEO Ammunition) manages demilitarization at seven Army depots and commercial firms, budgeting and funding the disposal of all munitions in the CAD stockpile. Joint Munitions Command (JMC) handles demilitarization of remaining conventional ammunition, while Army Aviation and Missile Command (AAMC) manages missiles and components. JMC provides logistics and sustainment support, oversees CAD stockpile storage, and arranges transportation. Military departments use inventory stratification reports to identify excess and deficiencies, with DOD Manual 4140.01 requiring stratification and reporting.

Annual inventory reports categorize ammunition into four types: requirement-related, economic retention, contingency retention, and potential reutilization/disposal. These reports help identify opportunities for redistribution, with the Office of the Executive Director for Conventional Ammunition comparing inventory data with procurement plans. Cross-leveling processes are used to offset individual procurements, with DOD avoiding $72 million in annual costs in 2014. Approximately 5 million items were transferred between services, with 3 million being small-caliber ammunition.

The Office of the Executive Director for Conventional Ammunition’s 2014 cross-leveling report indicated $104.2 million in cost avoidance. However, the Army’s annual stratification report did not include missile data managed by AAMC, leading to incomplete cross-leveling. Recommendations were made to include Army missiles in stratification reports, and by 2014, the Army provided missile information, enabling the transfer of 100 Javelin missiles from the Army to the Marine Corps, saving $3 million. Further recommendations included including unclaimed ammunition data.
