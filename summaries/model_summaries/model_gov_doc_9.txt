The Department of Energy (DOE) oversees a network of nuclear facilities established during World War II and the Cold War, primarily for research, production, and testing. These facilities house significant radioactive waste, including plutonium-contaminated sludge and hazardous chemicals, which have necessitated extensive cleanup efforts since the 1980s. The cleanup program, costing approximately $220 billion over 70 years, is governed by environmental laws such as CERCLA section 120 and RCRA amendments under the Federal Facility Compliance Act of 1992. DOE has completed cleanup at 74 of 114 sites, though the remaining sites present substantial challenges. Cleanup requires interagency cooperation between DOE, the EPA, and affected states, with site-specific treatment plans submitted for state approval.

The DOE’s cleanup program involves compliance agreements, issued by the EPA or state agencies, that require DOE to follow approved treatment plans. These agreements are categorized into three types: those mandated by CERCLA or RCRA, which apply to all major DOE sites and cover extensive cleanup activities; court-ordered settlements, which apply to a few sites and focus on specific issues; and agreements based on federal or state environmental laws, which address various cleanup tasks. The six largest DOE sites—Hanford, Savannah River, Idaho Falls, Rocky Flats, Oak Ridge, and Fernald—account for about two-thirds of DOE’s cleanup funding and have the majority of the milestones outlined in compliance agreements. These sites are critical to DOE’s cleanup program, which involves collaboration with federal and state agencies, citizen groups, and other stakeholders.

The cleanup program has made progress, with approximately 4,558 out of 7,186 milestones completed as of December 2001. Of the 4,262 milestones at the six largest sites, 2,901 were completed, with an average completion rate of 79% of the original deadlines. However, the completion rate varied significantly, from 95% at Rocky Flats to 47% at Savannah River. Over 1,334 milestones remain uncompleted, with more added in the future. While DOE has completed many milestones on time, this metric is not reliable as it often focuses on administrative tasks rather than actual cleanup work.
